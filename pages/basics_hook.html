Hooke's Law & Elastic Constants

Hooke’s law gives the relationship between stress and strain. Hooke’s law is valid for linear, elastic structure. The Hooke’s law in 1D is stated as: For small deformation, stress is directly proportional to strain. Mathematically σ=k ε where k is material constant.

Depending on type of stress, k can be of various types as given below:

Modulus of elasticity E

According to Hooke’s law, axial stress is directly proportional to axial strain, the proportionality being modulus of elasticity. Mathematically, σ=E ε.

Modulus of elasticity says how much stress is required to achieve unit strain. So higher modulus of elasticity means high stiffness of the material.

Poisson’s ratio (μ)

When a tensile force P is applied on a bar, its length increases, meaning longitudinal strain. At the same time diameter of bar decreases, meaning transverse strain. Poisson’s ratio is the ratio of transverse strain to longitudinal (or axial) strain.

The possible value of Poisson’s ratio is -1 to 0.5.

Modulus of rigidity (G) /Shear Modulus of elasticity

According to Hooke’s law, shear stress is directly proportional to shear strain, the proportionality being modulus of rigidity. Mathematically, τ=G γ

Modulus of rigidity says how much stress is required to achieve unit strain. So higher modulus of rigidity means high rigidity of the material.

Bulk Modulus of elasticity (K)

According to Hooke’s law, volumetric stress (or pressure) is directly proportional to volumetric strain, the proportionality being bulk modulus of elasticity. Mathematically, p=k ΔV/V

Bulk modulus measures the incompressibility of the material. Material with high K is less compressible than material with low K.

Relation between E, G & K

E=2G(1+μ)

E=3K(1-2μ)
